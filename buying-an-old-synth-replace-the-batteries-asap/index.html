Buying an old synth? Replace the batteries ASAP.

Mel

Don't delay, old batteries can eventually start leaking out and will ruin your synths, especially older ones. Replacing them, while usually not the hardest task, can be oddly challenging at times. Let's talk about the what, the why, and the how.

Batteries?!

Old synthesizers often used volatile memory to store sound information that's modified by the user, but a common problem with doing this is that you would lose that data upon powering off the device. You could use non-volatile storage, something like cassette tape, or a hard disk, but those were often slow and clumsy, compared to memory.

Enter batteries. This is a very clever solution - store it in a memory chip, but keep the memory chip powered at all times via a coin-cell battery. This way, the user can have the speed of blazing-fast RAM of the time, combined with the convenience of patch storage. This was the leading way to store programs, patches, performances and what-have-you on a ton of older workstations and synthesizers, with the other prominent way being floppy disks - remember those? ðŸ’¾

In short, batteries keep supplying power to where you would store your sound programs and patches, even when powered off, so you can access them at any point. Think of it like old SNES and Gameboy games doing save storage.

Obviously those won't last forever. You have to eventually open up your prized synth or game, and replace the battery, losing all of your hard work (hopefully you have it backed up!). Tangential, but batteries could also be used for a real-time clock, and that's why you have some copies of PokÃ©mon games that don't have a working clock.

Sure, you can live without saving or storage, but another, much bigger problem is leakage. Batteries build up gases inside that, if potent enough, can puncture through the metal casing. This doesn't normally occur if the device is actively used from manufacturing date, however we all know that doesn't really ever happen, synth and game alike. Therefore, you can totally end up with acid buildup, or worse, corrosion. So... if it's dead, or if it's old, why not just replace it?

Replacing a battery

This process can be either simple enough for anyone with a screwdriver to do, or requires a soldering iron (if it's soldered).

I'm focusing on the former, as I don't have a soldering iron yetÂ (I will have to use one for my QSR and S4 synths, more on that some other time).

Open up your synth, making sure it's not connected to power or anything, and look around and find where the battery is located. It should be pretty obvious where it is (if not, look for a coin-shaped object encased in some plastic holding it down), make note of what type of battery it is (CR2032s are common in synths, not so much in games), and either order or go out to buy a replacement.

Chances are you'll get them in bulk - perfect if you're like me and have multiple synths!

Doing it in bulk

...is exhausting as hell.

You should do this as soon as you get something new, because doing it in bulk, even if you're talking 4 or 5 devices at most, is tiring. You have to open each synth up, look for the battery, either pop it out and pop a new one in, or replace it via soldering, then screw it back on, THEN make sure it still works (it most likely will).

That alone can take around 15 minutes just for a single unit, and it'll create a big mess in your studio.

It is worth doing it if you haven't been able to before, but it is more worth doing it as your synths come (or go, if you're planning to sell any and haven't recently done this). Possibly, it'll take even more if you have to solder, but I'm not qualified to have a say yet.

The outcome of doing it in bulk though is very rewarding, and it also allows you to redo your cabling if it's a mess like mine is. Meh, mine's still not great, haha...

A little tidbit before I go - the battery in the thumbnail was one of four I replaced today, it was the U220 one. It's also the oldest synth I have, having been released in 1989, so that checks out I suppose.

Anywho, thanks for reading - see you soon.
